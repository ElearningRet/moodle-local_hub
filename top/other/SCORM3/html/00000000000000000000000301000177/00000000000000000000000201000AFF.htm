What Is a WAN?
This section defines wide-area networks, known as WANs. As you will learn, WANs are used to connect multiple LANs across geographical distance. This allows for e-learning to spread across a dispersed private network.
Wide-Area Networks

WANs are designed to operate between a large mixture of telecommunications carriers and typically allow access over serial interfaces operating at lower speeds.

WANs can be designed to provide part-time (dial-on demand) or full-time connectivity over wide, even global, areas. Frame Relay, SMDS, and X.25 are examples of WANs.

Example
These are examples of WANs:
 
  • An airline reservation system accesses information from the Mainframe via Dumb peripherals in many cities/locations.

  • A point of sale credit card validation uses X.25 and POTS.

Non-Example
A non-example of a WAN is a small office that uses PCs, printer(s), a file server, and a scanner.
Analogy
The WAN is like an interstate highway that connects many LANs or cities.
Impact on E-Learning
Learners in geographically dispersed areas can connect to the same e-learning experience. In addition to this wide-area delivery, an e-learning system connected to a WAN can track each learner's progress and store it in a centralized location. WANs, however, usually have limited bandwidth, which may limit the type or amount of multimedia and streaming video that can be delivered.
