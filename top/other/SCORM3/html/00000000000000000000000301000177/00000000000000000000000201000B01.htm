What Is Internetworking?
This section defines internetworks. As you will learn, internetworks are used to connect multiple networks that may use different protocols, owned by different companies, or be part of the public Internet. For e-learning, the Internet has removed barriers to content access between users and companies worldwide.
Internetworks

An internetwork moves information anywhere within a corporation and to external suppliers and customers. By serving as the organization's information highway, the internetwork has become a key strategic asset and a competitive advantage.

Routers
Devices called routers are a key element in internetworks because they allow (or deny) the communications between LANs and WANs.
Example
Companies have moved toward providing their training over the Internet. Employees (learners) may access their lessons at anytime and from anywhere.
Non-Example
Employees at a firm have desktop computers. They are all connected via a LAN. Employees who work at sites in other countries are unable to access the learning materials due to the limitations of the LAN.
Analogy
The Internet is like a highway that carries data from one point to another. It connects LANs like a highway system connects towns or cities.
Impact on E-Learning
Learners in geographically dispersed areas can connect to the same e-learning experience. Unlike a privately operated wide-area network (WAN), internetworking and the Internet allow for learners to find and access the same e-learning event. This allows companies to offer e-learning to others, and then deliver and track their progress.

The Internet, however, does have some limitations for the learner. The capabilities of the learner's computer, the bandwidth of the network connection, and language differences are three of many issues that impact e-Learning through internetworks.
