1960s-1970s: Mainframe Networks

In the 1960s and early 1970s, computer communication typically was organized in the form of dumb terminals connected to a host (mainframe). The processing power and much of the memory resided in the host as opposed to the terminals.

At this time, e-learning was proprietary. Given the networking environment, learners were taught the software that was housed on the mainframe.

Value-Added Networks
Value-added networks enabled access to applications on time-sharing hosts. The value added by time-sharing networks included password and login, multiplexing, and wide-scale, local-access telephone numbers into the network.
Impact on E-Learning
With the constraints of dumb terminals, low bandwidth, and proprietary nature of the mainframe-based programs, e-learning has some limitations. It is often limited to simply text with no multimedia, which is used to support the proprietary applications.

Therefore, the breadth and depth of the e-learning are greatly reduced when compared to other network architectures. However, this training is available to every terminal connected to the mainframe.
